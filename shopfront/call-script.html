Call cue cards

Open this before you dial. The orange-barred lines are words to say out loud; the highlighted [slots] are yours to fill in. It's a cue card, not a script — sound like yourself.

Call 1 — The cold call

Goal: they get the mockup link and you get permission to follow up. Not a sale. Two minutes, then out.

Before you dial
  • Know the owner's first name.
  • Have the mockup link ready to text on the spot.
  • Pick a quiet hour — not a barber's Saturday, not a café's 8–11 rush.
  • Stand up and smile; it carries down the line.
Opening — 15 seconds

"Hi — is that [their name]? Great. My name's [your name], I'm one of the two people behind Shopfront Studio, a small web studio here in [town]. Have you got thirty seconds? That's honestly all this is."

The hook

"We didn't want to call you with a sales pitch, so we built you something first. There's a free preview of what [business]'s website could look like, sitting ready right now. It cost you nothing — worst case, you look at it and tell us no."

The ask

"Can I take two minutes to walk you through it — or even easier, I'll text you the link right now and you look whenever the shop's quiet. What's the best number?"

The goal of this call is the link plus permission to follow up — not a sale. Once you have the number, confirm when you'll ring back, thank them, and hang up.

If they push back
They say

"Not interested."

"Completely fair — most people say that before they've seen it. Can I text you the link anyway? If it's not for you, delete it, and I won't call again."

They say

"I don't have time."

"Totally understand — sounds like I've caught you mid-rush. I'll text the link and get out of your hair. Is this the best number?"

They say

"How much is it?"

"Fixed price — $250 for a one-page site, no monthly fees, ever. But the preview is free either way, so have a look first and then decide if it's worth a chat."

They say

"We have a Facebook page."

"That's a good start, and plenty of people keep theirs. A website is what comes up when someone Googles '[business type] near me' — and it never buries your opening hours. The preview shows the difference better than I can."

They say

"Just send me something."

"Happy to — that's exactly what I'm offering. What's the best email or number? I'll send the preview today and give you a quick ring on [day] to hear what you thought."

Voicemail — 20 seconds

"Hi [their name], this is [your name] from Shopfront Studio in [town]. We build websites for local businesses, and we've already put together a free preview of one for [business] — no strings, I'd just love you to see it. I'll text the link to this number. If you like it, ring me back on [your number]. Either way, have a great day."

Don't do this
  • Don't argue. One no after they've heard the offer means thank them and hang up. The preview stays valid if they change their mind.
  • Don't talk tech. Say "shows up on Google" and "works on phones" — never anything jargon-flavored.
  • Don't discount on the phone. The fixed price is the pitch. If money's the blocker, offer the one-pager, not a deal.
  • Don't oversell. Two sentences, then the ask. The preview does the selling.
  • Don't read this page like a robot. It's a cue card, not a script.

Call 2 — The intake call

They're interested. Ten minutes of questions, tick as you go. Ticks clear when you reload the page.

Start here

"Thanks for making time — this is the easy part. Ten minutes of questions, then we go and build. There are no wrong answers and nothing to prepare."

The ten questions
Wrap up

Recap

"Let me play that back: you're [business], open [hours], the big thing about you is [their one line], and the site's main job is to get people to [call / book / visit]. Did I get that right?"

What happens next

"Here's what happens next: we build your free mockup and send it over in the next few days — usually two or three. You tell us what's wrong with it. Only when you love it do we talk about going ahead — no payment, nothing owed, until you've approved it."

Last line

"Text me those photos when you get a minute — and thank you, this was genuinely useful."
